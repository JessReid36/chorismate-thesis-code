# Methods — Reaction Path and Transition-State Location (Steps 15–18d)

*Draft Methods-chapter section covering the QM/MM reaction path for the frame-820 Michaelis-complex snapshot: endpoint optimisation, restrained scan, transition-state location, and IRC verification. Numbers are reproduced from the committed ORCA output.*

## Overview

Starting from a representative snapshot of the equilibrated Michaelis complex (frame 820), the chorismate-to-prephenate Claisen rearrangement was characterised at the QM/MM level as a single-barrier potential-energy path connecting an optimised reactant, a verified transition state, and an optimised product. The quantum region throughout comprised the 24 atoms of the chorismate substrate (residue CHA#2, formal charge −2, singlet), treated at B3LYP-D3BJ/def2-SVP with the RIJCOSX approximation and the def2/J auxiliary basis. The molecular-mechanical environment (ff14SB protein, GAFF substrate, TIP3P water, net-neutral with sodium counterions; 55,680 atoms in total) entered through electrostatic embedding, so that the full point-charge field polarised the QM density at every geometry. The reaction coordinate was defined throughout as the difference of the breaking and forming carbon distances, r = d(C4–O3) − d(C6–C1), where C4–O3 is the ether bond cleaved and C6–C1 the carbon–carbon bond formed in the pericyclic rearrangement; negative r denotes reactant-like (chorismate) geometries and positive r product-like (prephenate) geometries.

## Endpoint optimisation (Steps 15, 17)

The reactant endpoint was obtained by Cartesian L-BFGS optimisation (the L-Opt driver of the ORCA MM module) of a large, flexible active region defined in the manner of Claeyssens and co-workers: every residue with at least one atom within 12 Å of the substrate was allowed to relax (approximately 1,959 atoms), with the remainder of the system held fixed but retained in the embedding field. This active-region size was chosen deliberately to allow the active-site residues implicated in transition-state stabilisation to respond to the changing electronic structure of the substrate, rather than restricting relaxation to the substrate alone. The optimised reactant is unambiguously chorismate: the ether bond is intact and the forming contact is non-bonded (d(C4–O3) = 1.464 Å, d(C6–C1) = 3.251 Å, r = −1.787 Å).

The product endpoint was optimised separately and without restraint, seeded from the product-side terminus of the restrained scan (below) rather than by interpolation, so as to avoid the endpoint-interpolation artefact that had produced spurious bond-formation in an earlier attempt at this system. The optimised product is prephenate, with the ether bond broken and the new carbon–carbon bond formed (d(C4–O3) = 3.027 Å, d(C6–C1) = 1.580 Å, r = +1.448 Å). Optimising the two endpoints independently, rather than relaxing one interpolated chain, ensures that each terminus is a genuine minimum on the same potential-energy surface and that the subsequent path connects two physically correct states.

Relative to the optimised reactant (QM/MM energy −1076.129034 Ha), the optimised product (−1076.154898 Ha) lies 16.23 kcal/mol lower, i.e. the rearrangement is strongly exothermic at this level of theory. (This bias-free reaction energy is distinct from, and larger in magnitude than, the value read off the terminus of the restrained scan below, which carries the restraint contribution; the optimised-endpoint value is the thermodynamically meaningful one.)

## Restrained adiabatic scan (Step 16)

A coordinated restrained scan mapped the potential-energy profile between the endpoints. Because ORCA 6.0.x offers no composite difference-of-distances collective variable, the two component distances were restrained independently: harmonic restraints (spring constant 400 kJ mol⁻¹ Å⁻²) were placed on d(C4–O3) and d(C6–C1) and their targets stepped in tandem across twenty windows, each window restarting from the final geometry of the previous one. The optimisation within each window was performed by L-BFGS on the same active region as the endpoint optimisations. (A Cartesian L-BFGS optimiser accepts only Cartesian constraints; the internal-coordinate `%geom Scan` facility is silently ignored under L-Opt, which is why the reaction coordinate was enforced through restraints rather than a coordinate scan.)

The scan produced a clean single-barrier profile. The energy rose to a maximum of +16.37 kcal/mol at window 11 (r = −0.947 Å) and descended to the product basin, with the breaking and forming distances varying monotonically and the two chemically incorrect contacts (C1–C9 and C1–C10, the alternative carbons that a mis-assigned coordinate would have driven together) remaining non-bonded throughout — confirming that the coordinate drives the intended C6–C1 bond formation and not a spurious one. The scan maximum is a restrained, discretised estimate of the barrier and slightly overestimates it; its principal role here is to supply a validated reaction channel, a transition-state guess (the highest window), and a product seed for the unrestrained product optimisation.

## Transition-state location (Steps 18, 18b, 18c)

The transition state was located by nudged-elastic-band optimisation followed by saddle refinement, but the refinement required a change of active-region strategy that is worth setting out, because it bears on how transition states are properly located in large enzyme QM/MM systems.

An eight-image NEB-TS calculation was initialised by linear interpolation between the optimised reactant and product, with the highest scan window supplied as the transition-state guess. Linear interpolation was used rather than the image-dependent pair-potential (IDPP) scheme because the latter, being an O(N²) pairwise construction over all 55,680 atoms, did not complete; linear interpolation is valid here because the frozen environment is identical between the two endpoints and only the substrate and its immediate surroundings move along the path. The elastic band converged to a minimum-energy path in eighteen iterations, placing the climbing image 15.94 kcal/mol above the reactant image. The subsequent transition-state optimisation of the full ~1,959-atom active region, however, did not converge: the RMS gradient met its tolerance but the maximum gradient component plateaued (in the range 0.001–0.005 Eh Bohr⁻¹, against a 3 × 10⁻⁴ tolerance) while the barrier drifted slowly downward from 16.2 to 15.2 kcal/mol over roughly eighteen cycles. This is the expected behaviour of eigenvector-following saddle optimisation driven by an approximate Hessian over several thousand degrees of freedom: the reaction-core curvature is adequate, but the many soft environmental coordinates lack correct curvature information and prevent the maximum gradient from tightening. This behaviour, rather than any error in the path, is why a full unconstrained saddle optimisation of a large flexible region is not the standard route to an enzyme transition state.

Supplying an exact Hessian to guide the full-region optimisation was attempted but proved intractable on the available hardware: the numerical Hessian expanded to 1,666 displacements over roughly 278 atoms and the calculation exceeded the memory of the assigned node before converging. Its single completed cycle (barrier 15.48 kcal/mol; d(C4–O3) = 2.118, d(C6–C1) = 2.516, r = −0.398 Å) is nonetheless consistent with the reduced-region result below, and serves as an independent partial corroboration.

The transition state was therefore located by optimising a reduced active region under full electrostatic embedding, following the core/environment partition standard in microiterative enzyme QM/MM transition-state searches and matching the working active-region definition arrived at empirically in earlier work on this system. The movable region comprised the 24 substrate atoms together with the sidechains of the first-shell catalytic residues — three arginines (Arg90, Arg7, and the cross-subunit Arg63′), Glu78, and Tyr108 — for 102 atoms in total; the anticatalytic active-site lysine (Lys60) and all remaining atoms were held fixed but retained in the embedding field, so that transition-state stabilisation by the surrounding charge distribution remained fully present in the energy. The first-shell residues were identified by proximity of each residue copy to the substrate in the trimer, the active site being inter-subunit. The optimisation was seeded from the best NEB structure and given correct reaction-coordinate curvature through an exact Hybrid Hessian computed over the 24 substrate atoms only (144 displacements), the pericyclic imaginary mode being localised on the substrate.

This optimisation converged a genuine first-order saddle point in twenty-one cycles (QM/MM energy −1076.104998 Ha) at a potential-energy barrier of +15.08 kcal/mol relative to the optimised reactant. The transition-state geometry is symmetric with respect to the two reacting bonds and consistent with a concerted mechanism (d(C4–O3) = 2.111 Å, d(C6–C1) = 2.527 Å, r = −0.416 Å), with both incorrect carbon contacts clearly non-bonded (C1–C9 = 3.97 Å, C1–C10 = 3.13 Å). A partial Hessian vibrational analysis over the substrate confirmed the stationary point as a transition state, with a single dominant imaginary frequency of −313 cm⁻¹ corresponding to the coupled C4–O3 cleavage and C6–C1 formation; a second, negligible imaginary value below 30 cm⁻¹ is a numerical artefact of the partial Hessian and carries no physical significance. The imaginary frequency is close to the value obtained in independent prior work on this enzyme, indicating that the same transition state has been recovered through the present, documented route.

## Intrinsic reaction coordinate verification (Step 18d)

To confirm that the located saddle connects the intended reactant and product, an intrinsic reaction coordinate calculation was run in both directions from the transition state, reading the substrate Hessian for the initial displacement along the imaginary mode and following steepest descent under the same reduced active region and embedding. The two branches descend to opposite states: one terminates in the chorismate basin (d(C4–O3) = 1.483 Å, d(C6–C1) = 2.969 Å, r = −1.486 Å) and the other in the prephenate basin (d(C4–O3) = 2.827 Å, d(C6–C1) = 1.589 Å, r = +1.239 Å). The endpoints lie within the reactant and product basins rather than exactly at the optimised minima, as is expected for a steepest-descent path that halts on entering each basin; their unambiguous placement on opposite sides of the barrier confirms that the transition state connects chorismate and prephenate and is not an unrelated stationary point.

## Cross-validation and comparison to experiment

The barrier is consistent across three independent determinations: the restrained scan maximum (16.37 kcal/mol), the converged NEB climbing image (15.94 kcal/mol above the reactant image), and the converged reduced-region saddle (15.08 kcal/mol). (The full-region saddle refinement that follows the NEB path did not itself converge, drifting to about 15.2 kcal/mol before stalling, for the reasons set out above; the climbing-image value quoted here is from the converged elastic band.) This agreement across methods, together with the single imaginary mode and the IRC connectivity, establishes the transition state as a robust feature of the model for this snapshot rather than an artefact of any one algorithm.

For comparison with experiment, the appropriate reference for a single potential-energy barrier is the experimental activation enthalpy, ΔH‡ = 12.7 ± 0.4 kcal/mol, rather than the activation free energy, ΔG‡ = 15.4 kcal/mol, since a single static path does not contain the entropic and conformational-averaging contributions that separate the two. The computed 15.08 kcal/mol lies approximately 2.4 kcal/mol above ΔH‡: within the range reported for this enzyme at comparable B3LYP/MM levels (12.7–16.1 kcal/mol) but at its upper end, as is consistent with a single high-barrier conformer sampled from the reactant ensemble. The close numerical similarity between the computed barrier and the experimental ΔG‡ is coincidental — the two are different physical quantities — and is not treated as agreement. Quantitative comparison with the measured rate requires averaging over multiple reactant conformations and higher-level single-point corrections, both of which are pursued subsequently; the density functional and basis set used here are known to underestimate the intrinsic barrier, so the absolute value is provisional and the internal consistency across methods is the stronger claim at this stage.
